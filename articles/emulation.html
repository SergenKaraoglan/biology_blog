The Pulse of the Machine

Every processor is a heartbeat. In each cycle, it fetches an opcode from memory, decodes what it means, and executes the logic. To emulate a CPU, we must recreate this cycle in software, often with a giant `switch` statement or a lookup table.

The Geometry of Information

A CPU doesn't know what a "controller" or "screen" is. It only knows memory addresses. Through Memory-Mapped I/O, writing to a specific address like `0x2000` might actually be talking to the graphics chip. We must build a virtual "Bus" that routes data to the correct device.

Drawing with Electrons

Retro consoles didn't have "framebuffers" in the modern sense. They drew to the screen one scanline at a time, racing the electron beam of a CRT. Emulating a PPU (Picture Processing Unit) requires precise timing to match the horizontal and vertical synchronization of old displays.

The Console That Started It All

The Atari 2600 (1977) is the Rosetta Stone of console emulation. Its architecture is spartan: a MOS 6507 CPU (a pin-reduced, cost-cut version of the legendary 6502, limited to a 13-bit address bus and therefore 8KB of addressable space), a RIOT chip providing 128 bytes of RAM, two 8-bit I/O ports, and a programmable interval timer, and the TIA — the Television Interface Adapter — responsible for all video and audio output.

The TIA is one of the most constrained graphics chips ever designed. There is no framebuffer. The entire visible display must be generated on-the-fly, scanline by scanline, by "racing the beam". The CPU has roughly 76 machine cycles per scanline to set up the TIA's registers before the electron gun sweeps across the screen. Miss the window, and the image tears.

The TIA draws using five movable objects: two Player sprites (8 pixels wide each), two Missile sprites (1–8 pixels), one Ball (1–8 pixels), and a 20-bit Playfield pattern that can be reflected or repeated to fill the full 160-pixel-wide scanline. Colour is controlled by dedicated registers — COLUPF for the playfield, COLUP0/COLUP1 for the players, and COLUBK for the background — drawn from the 128-colour NTSC palette.
