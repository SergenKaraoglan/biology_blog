1. Charge & Potential

At the center of electronic engineering is the electron. Electricity is not a mystical liquid, but the coordinated movement of charged particles. When we create a "potential difference" (Voltage), we are essentially creating a pressure that encourages electrons to move from a place of high density to a place of low density.

Imagine a pipe filled with marbles. To move the marbles, you must push from one end. In a circuit, the battery provides that push, and the conductor (like copper) provides the pipe.

2. Ohm's Law: The Balancing Act

The relationship between Voltage (V), Current (I), and Resistance (R) is the bedrock of electronics. This is defined by Ohm's Law: V = I × R.

6. Transistors: The Universal Valve

The most important invention of the 20th century is the Transistor. It is a solid-state device that can act as both an amplifier and a switch. By using a minuscule amount of charge at the "Base" terminal, we can control a much larger current flowing between the "Collector" and "Emitter".

This simple "control" mechanism allows us to build Logic Gates. If we can use one circuit to turn another circuit ON or OFF, we can perform computation.

7. Building Logic from Physics

When you combine transistors, you transcend physics and enter the realm of information. An AND gate only outputs a signal if both its inputs are active. By chaining these basic operations, we can build adders, memory, and eventually, the processor you are using to read this.
